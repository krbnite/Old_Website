Center for Solar-Terrestrial Research, New Jersey Institute of Technology (NJIT)

Consultant / Research Scientist

September 2010 - August 2011

 

At the Center for Solar-Terrestrial Research, I have investigated various issues under the umbrella of "space weather." Most of my research has been concerned with how to characterize the state of the magnetosphere via knowledge of its open-closed boundary (OCB)---that is, the boundary between lower-latitude closed terrestrial field lines and field lines in the polar cap regions that have been reconnected to the interplanetary magnetic field (IMF). During geomagnetic events, the OCB can vary in both size and shape; the extent of this variability is a vital component of space weather research and modeling.

 

In order to monitor the OCB and study the magnetosphere, I utilize large data sets gathered at 1-Hz by a high-latitude network of fluxgate magnetometers. Lanzerotti et al [1999] demonstrated that spectral analysis of a fluxgate's data (on the dayside) yields information specifying when/if the OCB crossed the fluxgate. My work has been to extend this analysis to the nightside and ultimately develop a scheme that continuously determines if the fluxgate is equatorward of, poleward of, or on the OCB. By employing a strategically-designed network topology spanning a range of high geomagnetic latitudes and magnetic local times, in this way we are able to isolate the structure and dynamics of the OCB before and during various geomagnetic conditions, such as during the passage co-rotating interaction region (CIR) or coronal mass ejection (CME).

Department of Mathematical Sciences, NJIT

Research with Denis Blackmore

2008 - Present

 

Dr. Blackmore and I are currently investigating topics in fractional calculus and fractional differential equations with an eye towards applications to field theories in physics. We hope to finish up significant portions of our investigations over the coming months and are working towards publishing these results before I leave for my PhD studies.

 

Presently, I have authored two papers with Dr. Blackmore, one of which is now in press in the Journal of Mechanics of Materials and Structures and another which has been submitted Condensed Matter Physics. The paper in press is the first of many in an ongoing investigation into the dynamical behavior of  various configurations of granular particles subject to gravity and a sequence of small, periodically applied taps using an approximate continuum model developed by Dr. Blackmore in previous work. This model, which comprises an inﬁnite-dimensional dynamical system and is now known as a BSR field, is studied with a focus on nonlinear wave type behavior. Tying together our work on granular flow dynamics and fractional field theories, we will be looking into fractionalized versions of the BSR field. The paper submitted to Condensed Matter Physics (“Integrability Analysis of Regular and Fractional BSR Fields”) is our first step in this direction.

Center for Solar-Terrestrial Research, NJIT

Graduate Research Assistant

September 2008 - August 2010

 

High above the earth’s lower and middle atmosphere there exists a spread of dynamical conditions characterizing what has become known as space weather. In the manner that “weather” refers to the evolving state of the troposphere, the phrase “space weather” denotes the state of the near-space environment—often referred to as geospace—which includes the ionosphere and magnetosphere. Depending on context, space weather also refers to the state of the earthward flowing solar wind and conditions at the surface of the sun.

 

A corotating interaction region (CIR) is a distinct type of solar wind configuration and one of the primary drivers of space weather. When a CIR crosses paths with the earth’s magnetosphere, inherent Alfvenic structures within it cause the magnetosphere to undergo vigorous oscillations and deformations. During most phases of solar activity there is little hope in isolating the exact nature of this interaction. Fortunately, the year 2008 marked a historically quiet period of solar activity. Dr. Andrew Gerrard and I took this opportunity to clearly distinguish the spectral signature of the CIR and isolate its impact on the magnetosphere by using the PENGUIn AGOs network of ground-based magnetometers on the Antarctic continent; the data was subsequently analyzed using a host of programs/techniques developed in both MATLab and IDL.

Observational Cosmology Laboratory, NASA, Goddard Space Flight Center

Intern

 

My team developed a proposal for an instrument to measure the polarization pattern and black body spectrum of the cosmic microwave background and thereby test the predictions of inflationary physics. Specifically, the instrument was designed to detect a unique signal produced by theoretical gravity waves that are predicted to have been excited during the proposed inflationary epoch. I programmed simulations concerned with how the instrument would collect and interpret data while in orbit and revolving about its own axis.
