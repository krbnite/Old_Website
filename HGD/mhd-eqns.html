Mass Continuity (aka Conservation of Mass) $$\partial_{t}\rho+\nabla\cdot\left(\rho\mathbf{v}\right)= 0$$ $$\partial_{t}\rho+\nabla\cdot\left(\rho\mathbf{v}\right)= 0$$ The rate of change of mass in a volume is due solely to the divergence of mass fluxes (aka momentum density, or just momentum when speaking loosely) through the surface of the volume.
Cauchy Momentum (aka Euler's Equation, aka Newton's 2nd Law) $$\rho D_{t}\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{j}\times\mathbf{B} - \nabla{P} -\nabla \Phi_{G} + \nu\nabla^{2}\mathbf{v}$$
  • Oftentimes the fluid is considered inviscid (\(\nu = 0\)) and so the viscous force drops out.
  • The graviational term can often be ignored since contributions from within the plasma region are often negligible compared to sources outside (e.g., the mass of the ionosphere versus the mass of the Earth below it); when the self-gravitating terms are negligible, the gravity term is decoupled from the fluid equations.
  • \(D_{t}\) is the convective derivative: \(D_{t} = \partial_{t}+\mathbf{v}\cdot\nabla\)
Equation of State $$D_{t}\left(P\rho^{-\gamma}\right)=0$$
  • The equation of state is prescribed and can be a weak point in a model.
  • Often the ideal gas law is used, but with caution and foresight that it is a built in potential source of error: \(P=\frac{NRT}{V}=nk_{B}T=\rho(\gamma-1)\epsilon\), where N is the number of moles and R is the gas constant.
  • The equation of state allows one to relate state variables like pressure, temperature, and mass density. Pressure,\(P\), and mass density,\(\rho\), are often considered the most useful to keep track of, while, e.g., temperature and entropy are regarded as functions of pressure and mass density and can be derived from the equation of state if necessary.
Gauss' Law $$\nabla\cdot\mathbf{E}=\mathfrak{q}/\epsilon=0$$ $$\nabla\cdot\mathbf{E}=4\pi\mathfrak{q}$$ Typically, Gauss' law would be written \(\nabla\cdot\mathbf{E}=\mathfrak{q}/\epsilon\), where \(\mathfrak{q}\) is the electric charge density. However, in MHD, the fluid is considered quasi-neutral. That is, although are isolated charges on the smallest scales, at the MHD scale, these charges balance out to zero.
Gauss's Law for magnetic fields $$\nabla\cdot\mathbf{B}=0$$ $$\nabla\cdot\mathbf{B}=0$$ The magnetic field is always nonconservative (aka solenoidal).
Faraday's Law $$\nabla\times\mathbf{E}=-\partial_{t}\mathbf{B}$$ $$\nabla\times\mathbf{E}=-\frac{1}{c}\partial_{t}\mathbf{B}$$ Often written \(\partial_{t}\mathbf{B}=-\nabla\times\mathbf{E}\).
The Maxwell-Ampere Law $$\nabla\times\mathbf{B}=\mu\mathbf{j}+\frac{1}{c^2}\partial_{t}\mathbf{E}$$ $$\nabla\times\mathbf{B}=\frac{\mu}{4\pi}\mathbf{j}+\frac{1}{c}\partial_{t}\mathbf{E}$$ For emphasis on time evolution, this can be rewritten:
\(\partial_{t}\mathbf{E}= c^{2}\nabla\times\mathbf{B} - \mu c^{2}\mathbf{j}\)

Furthermore, in most applications \(\partial_{t}\mathbf{E}/c^2 \sim 0\) and so this becomes Ampere's law, \(\nabla\times\mathbf{B}=\mu\mathbf{j}\)
Ohm's Law $$\mathbf{j}=\sigma\left(\mathbf{E}+\mathbf{v}\times\mathbf{B}\right)$$ This can be shown to be zero (or nearly zero) under certain conditions. When this is the case, Ohm's law sometimes comes in disguise as \(\mathbf{E}=-\mathbf{v}\times \mathbf{B}\)
More on the Equation of State
  • In absence of non-adiabatic heating (e.g., viscous dissipation) and cooling (e.g., radiation, conduction), Ds/Dt=0 (s: specific entropy); that is, fluid elements then undergo reversible thermodynamic changes, preserving their entropy (this is not always a good assumption, e.g., shocks violate this).
  • Polytropic gas: an ideal gas with constant \(C_{v}\), \(C_{p}\), \(\gamma\), and mean molecular weight.
  • Polytropic index, n: \(\gamma = 1 + \frac{1}{n}\)
  • Equipartition of energy for classical gas with N DOFs per particle gives \(\gamma=1+\frac{2}{N}\)
  • classical monatomic gas w N=3: polytropic w gamma=5/3, n=3/2
  • specific internal energy, e = P/(gamma-1)rho
  • barotropic: fluid whose denisity is a function of pressure only
  • ideal, isentropic gas: P=(cs^2)rho (cs=const=isothermal sound speed)
  • isentropic, polytropic: P=Krho^gamma
  • incompressible fluid: Drho/Dt=0, implying \nabla\cdot\mathbf{v}=0
  • can be found formally by taking limit gamma->inf
  • incompressibility eliminates acoustic phenomena
  • ideal gas law not valid at very high densities


Conservative Form

In addition to seemingly missing variables, one might be stumped occasionally when seeing the MHD equations written in a way that seems alien. Of course, which presentation of the equations looks alien depends on which book or set of notes you studied first. Above I wrote the equations in non-conservative form. Many people do. Here I write the equations in conservative form. Again, many people do. Know both. They're equivalent. It is just a matter of taste. What is nice about the conservative form is that it emphasizes the nonlinear or tensorial nature of some of the quantities.

Conservation laws are written in the following form (a la slides by Nick Murphy):

\(\partial_{t}(stuff)+\nabla\cdot(\)flux of stuff\()=0\)

Source and sink terms, if any, would go on the right-hand side. If "stuff" is a scalar, then the "flux of stuff" is a vector. If "stuff" is a vector, then the "flux of stuff" is a dyadic tensor. If this equation is satisfied for a quantity, then that quantity is locally conserved.

Magnetic Flux Conservation $$\partial_{t}\mathbf{B} + \nabla\times\mathbf{E}=0 \\ \nabla\cdot\mathbf{B}=0$$ The flux through a surface is proportional to the electric field at the boundary of that surface. If the simple form of Ohm's law is applicalbe where \(\mathbf{E}=-\mathbf{v}\times\mathbf{B}\), then this equation is: \(\partial_{t}\mathbf{B} - \nabla\times\left(\mathbf{v}\times\mathbf{B}\right) = 0\)

Eulerian Set vs Lagrangian Set

Above I mixed derivatives in the equations at my convenience. However, in fluid dynamics, one often finds the equations written using either the Lagrangian (convective view point) or the Eulerian (stationary view point) derivative in isolation. This is to emphasize the physical meaning of the equations set up in one reference frame or another. I think this possibly mucks up the equations since Faraday's law and the Maxwell-Ampere law are not typically written in a Lagrangian way.

To be clear:

  • Eulerian View: observer fixes eye on a volume element of space; parameters are derived with respect to this location as fluid moves through it.
  • Lagrangian View: observer fixes eye on a volume element of fluid, which moves through space (this is the "fluid's point of view"); parameters are derived with respect to this moving fluid element.

Reduced Set

Oftentimes it appears as if the MHD equations are missing a few key variables. Specifically, one might wonder what happened to the electric field, the current density, and sometimes even the pressure. These quantities have not disappeared! Given certain experimentally verifiable or reasonably applicable conditions, the number of independent variables in the full set of equations is reduced. One may still use the full set, but it becomes easier and more tractable to reduce the set of equations by solving for some of the variables in terms of the other variables. At any stage then (usually at the end of any derivations), one may rewrite the full set of equations from the reduced set using the conditional equations prescribed at the onset of the problem.

There are a few conditions that are widely applicable in MHD applications: the low-frequency approximation (changes in the electric field are small relative to other parameters of the plasma), the inviscid fluid approximation (the plasma is nothing like molasses), and the adiabatic equation of state (the plasma is considered inviscid—that is, to have zero viscosity—and so the viscosity term in Newton's second law drops out.

Plasma Regimes

Plasmas are extremely complex, so approximations are often made to study plasmas in simplified settings. For a given set of approximations, a "plasma regime" is defined where those approximations hold valid.
  • Unmagnetized Plasma: a plasma where there is no background magnetic field (i.e., no "guide field"); for \(\mathbf{B}=\mathbf{B_{0}}+\mathbf{B_{1}}\), where \(\mathbf{B_{0}}\) is considered the equilibrium (or mean, or background) field, and \(\mathbf{B_{1}}\) is considered the magnetic fluctuations of the plasma, an unmagnetized plasma has \(\mathbf{B_{0}}=0\).
  • Electrostatic Plasma: plasmas for which the assumption of no inductive electric fields is valid (that is, where \(\partial_{t}\mathbf{B} = -\nabla\times\mathbf{E} = 0\)).
  • Low Frequency Approximation: assume that the displacement current in the Maxwell-Ampere law can be neglected (\(\partial_{t}\mathbf{E}/c^{2} \approx 0\)).
