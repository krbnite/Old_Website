Reference: Maxwell's Equations (UNITS: MKS)

Maxwell's equations come in many forms, which can seem unrelated or alien to the newcomer. For example, one might see Faraday's law written for circuits one day, for magnetofluids the next, and then again in the context of general relativity.

Furthermore, although the equations are often written in the MKS system of units, they are just as frequently written in the CGS system of units, resulting in similar looking equations with different coefficients (in the relativistic setting, one might find other systems of units being employed as well, like the natural units).

Note that Maxwell's equations, in general (even the microscopic set), do not fully explain electromagnetic phenomena. They are but an approximation, despite being an overwhelmingly amazing approximation for most applications. A refinement of Maxwell's equations comes from Quantum Electrodynamics. One might see the relevant QED equations referred to as Maxwell's equations from time to time; in this case, what is typically called Maxwell's equations might then be referred to as the classical Maxwell equations. The QED equations are overkill for most applications since quantum deviations from the classical Maxwell equations are very small on everyday application scales, but they are included on this page to be thorough.

The reference pages on Maxwell's equations are simply meant to put a bunch of this stuff all on one place, and to give some notes on how to convert one set into another set, etc.

This page looks at the differential (localized) formulation of Maxwell's equations. For the ingegral (regional) forumulation, see this page.

This page does not look at QED, GR, or mathier representations (e.g., differential forms, geometric algebra, etc) than vector calculus.

Microscopic Media

These equations are sometimes referred to as Maxwell's equations in vacuum, or more often than not just "Maxwell's equations." They take into account the total charge and total current in a system, including any and all of the shennanigans and complications at the atomic scale. This set of equations can theoretically be used for everything, although this would not be an easy way to do things (the "macroscopic" equations given below define two auxillary fields that approximate the information resulting from the shennanigans and complications at the atomic level, reducing the complexity of the equation set).

Macroscopic Media

The "mascropic Maxwell equations" are also known as "Maxwell's equations in matter." These reduce to the "microscopic Maxwell equations" (the more familiar form of Maxwell's equations) in a vacuum or a linear, isotropic material.

The macroscopic decompose the total charge and total current into free and bound charges and currents: $$ \mathfrak{q} = \mathfrak{q}_{f}+\mathfrak{q}_{b} \\ \mathbf{j} = \mathbf{j}_{f}+\mathbf{j}_{b} $$

This allows for easier computation of the dynamically evolving fields, but at the cost of introducing two auxillary fields \(\mathbf{D} and \mathbf{H}\), often called the "displacement field" and the "magnetizing field," respectively. Then, depending on the material under study, phenomenological equations called the constitutive relations are determined to relate the auxillary fields to the fundamental fields (often times, the fields are linearly related by a tensor, or even just a scalar).

The constitutive relations are empirical formulations of the auxillary fields.

Gauss' Law$$ \nabla\cdot\mathbf{D} = \mathfrak{q}_{f}$$
Gauss' Law for Magnetic Fields $$ \nabla\cdot\mathbf{B}=0$$
Faraday's Law $$\nabla\times\mathbf{E} = -\partial_{t}\mathbf{B}$$
Maxwell-Ampere Law $$\nabla\times\mathbf{H}=\mathbf{j}_{f}+\partial_{t}\mathbf{D}$$
The Auxiallary Field Definitions
Displacement Field $$\mathbf{D}=\epsilon_{0}\mathbf{E}+\mathbf{\mathscr{P}}$$
Magnetizing Field $$\mathbf{H}=\frac{1}{\mu_{0}}\mathbf{B}-\mathbf{\mathscr{M}}$$
Free and Bound Sources
Total Charge $$\mathfrak{q} = \mathfrak{q}_{f}+\mathfrak{q}_{b}$$
Total Current $$\mathbf{j} = \mathbf{j}_{f}+\mathbf{j}_{b}$$
Bound Charge $$\mathfrak{q}_{b} = -\nabla\cdot\mathbf{\mathscr{P}}$$
Bound Current $$\mathbf{j}_{b} = \nabla\times\mathbf{\mathscr{M}}+\partial_{t}\mathbf{\mathscr{P}}$$
Constitutive Relations
Non-Dielectric, Non-magnetic Materials $$\mathbf{D}=\epsilon_{0}\mathbf{E} \\ \mathbf{H}=\frac{1}{\mu_{0}}\mathbf{B} $$
Isotropic, Linear Materials $$\mathbf{D}=\epsilon\mathbf{E} \\ \mathbf{H}=\frac{1}{\mu}\mathbf{B} $$
For more info on the constitutive relations, see the wiki page.

Relationship to the integral formulation

In single variable calculus, you might have learned the fundamental theorem of calculus (hopefully). At one point, you may have also heard about Green's theorem on the plane. In vector calculus, you might have learned of the divergence theorem. These are all really the same thing: instances of Stoke's theorem.
